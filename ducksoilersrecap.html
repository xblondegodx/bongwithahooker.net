Ducks Almost Manage To Take Flight, Oilers Spoil Party

April 20, 2026 · 9:30 PM

After eight long years, the Ducks are back in the playoffs. Helped by a laughably weak Pacific Division and a young roster with a couple veterans thrown in for flavor, this team almost stole Game 1 right in front of an anxious Edmonton crowd. These Oilers, who have history with the Ducks, had other ideas.

For much of the first period, the Ducks looked intimidated by their opponents if not plainly outmatched. They were outshot 14-4 and Lukas Dostal was left to fend for himself, doing his best to keep the puck out against a dangerous McDavid-Draisaitl duo. Eventually, the Oilers broke through and put two past Dostal to take a 2-0 lead into the first intermission. The Ducks looked cooked, early.

Whatever the Ducks heard during the first intermission worked. They came out looking like a different team, applying pressure to the Oilers and immediately getting Troy Terry an open look that he made good on. A great wing in the mold of Kariya and Perry (not coincidentally two of my favorite players).

From there, the Ducks shut down any attack McDavid and Draisaitl could generate. They were also checking Edmonton hard, not giving up the slot or the neutral zone; every inch of ice was being paid for. The Ducks suddenly looked like a veteran squad, forcing turnovers and capitalizing on mistakes. Leo Carlson gets the puck off a Dostal deflection and puts it away to tie the game.

As if they needed any help at this point, or perhaps because of his frustration with them not needing his help, hometown hero Darnell Nurse commits a costly penalty and gets two minutes in the box; the Ducks immediately score. (Terry again!) This young roster is showing potential. Dostal can hang in the net against a team that’s been to the Stanley Cup Finals the last two seasons.

At this point, the shot total is 26-17, Edmonton; the Ducks have outshot the Oilers 13-12 in the second period and scored three unanswered goals.

Edmonton pushed back late in the second, including a shot off the crossbar that sounded like it wanted to go in. The Ducks held on and were able to take a 3-2 lead into the third period. It did feel like the entire game had shifted, even if the Oilers were still generating chances, especially on one-timers, but not finishing them. Perhaps the Oilers simply knew if they generated chances, goals would come.

Early in the third, the Ducks continued to lock down the Oilers top line trio of McDavid, Draisaitl, and Mattias Ekholm; but it would be Radko the Butcher’s fall that would bring on the death spiral. After Bennett Sennecke almost scored on a clean chance to make it 4-2, the Oilers took it the other way. Dostal would deflect a shot but Gudas, going down without contact, left Dickinson unmarked for an easy goal. 3-3, with less than ten minutes to play.

After a quiet second period, Edmonton was awake. The crowd had life again; a lot of it.

To their credit, an inexperienced Ducks team with a handful of playoff games between most of them didn’t fold, and continued to make chances—Leo Carlson almost had his second goal to give the Ducks another lead but a pileup in front of goal led to naught.

Then, heartbreak. With less than two minutes to go, Draisaitl somehow got the puck through heavy coverage; Dostal had no chance to make a save as Kasperi Kapanen scored. 4-3 Oilers.

Dostal made way to the bench for an extra attacker but the Ducks couldn’t score. A wide open look at the net with 13 seconds left went wide, and the Ducks lost.

After eight years out of the playoffs, the Ducks are back, and down 1-0 to an old foe.
